Cold Composting

How to Compost Using the Cold Composting Method

  1. Choose a Composting Site: Select a dry, shady spot in your yard, ideally near a water source for easy access.
  2. Gather Materials: Collect kitchen scraps (fruits and vegetables, coffee grounds, eggshells) and yard waste (grass clippings, leaves, small branches). Include both nitrogen-rich greens and carbon-rich browns, but the exact ratio is less critical than in hot composting.
  3. Create a Compost Pile or Bin: You can use a compost bin or simply create a pile on the ground. Start with a layer of coarse materials (like twigs or straw) to promote drainage.
  4. Layer Your Materials: Add materials in layers, alternating greens and browns. Aim for a mix but don’t stress about perfect ratios. Avoid large clumps of similar materials; break them up if necessary.
  5. Moisture Management: Ensure the pile is moist but not waterlogged. If it gets too dry, sprinkle water; if too wet, add more browns.
  6. Let It Decompose Naturally: Unlike hot composting, cold composting doesn’t require turning the pile. Simply let it sit. Decomposition will occur over several months to a year.
  7. Monitor Progress: Check the pile occasionally for moisture and add more materials as needed. You can mix in new materials at any time.
  8. Harvest the Compost: After several months, the compost will break down into dark, crumbly material. This can take anywhere from 6 months to a year or longer. Sift through the pile to remove any large, undecomposed items and return them to the composting process.
  9. Use Your Compost: Mix the finished compost into your garden beds, use it as mulch, or incorporate it into potting soil.

Cold Composting Tips

  1. Choose a Location: Select a well-drained spot in your garden, ideally with some shade to prevent excessive drying out.
  2. Gather Materials: Use a mix of "greens" (nitrogen-rich materials like kitchen scraps, grass clippings, and green leaves) and "browns" (carbon-rich materials like dried leaves, cardboard, and straw).
  3. Layering: Alternate layers of greens and browns to create a balanced mixture. Aim for a ratio of about 2:1 browns to greens.
  4. Chop Ingredients: Cut or shred larger materials to help them break down faster. Smaller pieces decompose more quickly.
  5. Aeration: Turn the pile occasionally (every few months) to introduce air, which speeds up decomposition. If you prefer minimal effort, just let it sit—cold composting works well even without frequent turning.
  6. Moisture Control: Keep the compost pile damp, but not soggy. If it’s too dry, add water; if too wet, add more browns to soak up excess moisture.
  7. Patience: Cold composting takes longer than hot composting, often several months to a year. Be patient and let nature do its work.
  8. Watch for Pests: If pests become an issue, bury food scraps deeper in the pile or cover them with browns to deter them.
  9. Use a Bin or Pile: You can use a compost bin for a tidier appearance or simply create a pile. Just make sure it’s at least 3 feet wide and high for effective decomposition.
  10. Harvesting: Once the compost looks dark and crumbly and has an earthy smell, it’s ready to use in your garden.
