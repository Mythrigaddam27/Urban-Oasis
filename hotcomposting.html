COMPOST STRATEGIES

HOT COMPOSTING

How to Compost

  1. Choose a Composting Site: Find a well-drained area in your yard that gets some sun. Consider using a compost bin or simply a designated area in your garden.
  2. Gather Materials:
    • Greens (Nitrogen-rich): Kitchen scraps (vegetable peels, coffee grounds, eggshells), fresh grass clippings, and green leaves.
    • Browns (Carbon-rich): Dry leaves, straw, cardboard, paper, and wood chips. Aim for a ratio of about 2:1 browns to greens.
  3. Layering: Start with a layer of coarse materials (like twigs or straw) to promote airflow. Alternate layers of greens and browns, maintaining the 2:1 ratio. Keep the pile at least 3 feet wide and tall to generate heat.
  4. Moisture Management: Ensure the compost is moist but not soggy. A good rule of thumb is that it should feel like a damp sponge. If it's too dry, add water; if too wet, add more browns.
  5. Aerate the Pile: Turn the pile every 1-2 weeks using a pitchfork or shovel to introduce oxygen. This helps maintain high temperatures and speeds up the composting process.
  6. Monitor Temperature: Use a compost thermometer to check the internal temperature. Aim for 130°F to 160°F (54°C to 71°C) to kill pathogens and weed seeds. Adjust aeration and moisture based on temperature readings.
  7. Composting Time: Hot composting can take anywhere from 2 to 6 weeks, depending on the materials used, temperatures, and management.
  8. Finish the Compost: When the pile has shrunk significantly, is dark brown, and has an earthy smell, it’s ready. You can sift out larger pieces that haven't broken down and return them to the next compost batch.
  9. Using Your Compost: Mix the finished compost into garden beds, use it as mulch, or incorporate it into potting soil.
